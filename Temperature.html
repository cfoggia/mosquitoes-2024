    Temperature

    Temperature significantly influences mosquito behavior, reproduction, and their ability to transmit diseases. Mosquitoes are most active in warm temperatures ranging from 20–30°C (68–86°F), as these conditions enhance their flying, feeding, and biting rates. However, extreme heat exceeding 35°C (95°F) may reduce activity due to dehydration risks, while cooler temperatures below 10°C (50°F) can cause dormancy or death in most species. Reproduction is also temperature-dependent, with warmer conditions speeding up egg and larval development. For example, eggs can hatch within a day, and larvae grow faster in water temperatures between 20–30°C, though excessively hot water can be lethal. Temperature also impacts the transmission of mosquito-borne diseases. Pathogens like malaria and viruses such as dengue or Zika replicate more efficiently inside mosquitoes at higher temperatures, reducing the incubation period required for transmission. Malaria transmission, for instance, is most effective when temperatures are around 25–30°C but becomes unlikely below 16°C. Additionally, rising global temperatures due to climate change are expanding mosquito habitats into regions previously too cold for them, including higher altitudes and northern latitudes. In summary, while warm temperatures support mosquito activity and disease transmission, extreme heat or cold can limit their survival and reproduction. This relationship between temperature and mosquito behavior highlights the importance of monitoring climate changes and their potential impact on public health.
